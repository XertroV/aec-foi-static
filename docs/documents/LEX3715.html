3. Main Content from Released FOI Request Documents

The released (edited or full) documents provide insights into various operational and financial aspects of the AEC, particularly concerning the 2022 Federal Election and preparedness for future electoral events, including a potential referendum:

  • Document No. 22: Referendum: Funding arrangements for referendums
    • Funding for referendums and associated YES/NO campaigns is provided via government appropriation. The October 2022-23 Budget included $75.1 million over two years for preparatory work for a First Nations Voice to Parliament Referendum, with specific allocations for AEC preparations ($50.2 million) and increasing First Nations enrolment/participation ($16.1 million). An additional $160 million is provisioned in the Contingency Reserve.
  • Document No. 24: Financial matters
    • Highlights ongoing challenges with the AEC's funding model, noting a $24.5 million loss in 2021-22 (compared to a $47.9 million surplus in 2020-21). The AEC's operating results can fluctuate significantly due to external factors impacting expenditure timing, posing risks for managing complex electoral events and electoral roll growth.
  • Document No. 25: Vendors, Procurements and Major Tenders
    • The AEC undertakes extensive preparation and planning for procurements and tenders for federal electoral events, adhering to Commonwealth Procurement Rules (CPRs). A specific "Referendum Procurement Plan" is in place for a potential 2023 referendum, tracking critical procurements and contracts.
  • Document No. 26: Performance Statements
    • The AEC's 2021-22 Annual Performance Statements indicate that 74% (14 of 19) of performance measures were met, 5% (1 of 19) were "on track to be met," and 21% (4 of 19) were "partly met" (e.g., voter turnout, staff training completion). No measures were rated "not met."
  • Document No. 27: Travel
    • Total travel expenditure increased significantly to $5.393 million in 2021-22 (from $1.579 million in 2020-21 and $1.699 million in 2019-20), primarily due to the 2022 federal election. The AEC adheres to the Whole of Australian Government Travel Policy.
  • Document No. 28.2: Indigenous voter services and engagement
    • The AEC is committed to increasing First Nations electoral participation. The estimated national Indigenous enrolment rate reached 84.5% as of December 2022, a consistent increase from 74.7% in June 2017. Efforts include successful direct enrolment trials (adding ~15,500 First Nations people), targeted advertising campaigns, and the Indigenous Electoral Participation Program (partnerships, remote enrolment, culturally appropriate services). The AEC is also expanding remote polling services.
  • Document No. 36.2: Referendum Preparedness
    • The AEC, as an independent statutory authority, is responsible solely for the "machinery" of a referendum (not the campaigns). With appropriate lead time and funding, the AEC is ready to conduct a referendum, with costs estimated to be similar to a federal election (e.g., the 2022 federal election was budgeted at approximately $420 million).
  • Document No. 37.2: COVID safety measures – 2022 FE
    • The AEC safely delivered the 2022 federal election with COVID-19 safety measures in place, adapting to the changing environment without a reported spike in cases attributable to the election. Planning adhered to existing legislation (which does not allow for 100% postal or electronic voting), with additional staff, vaccination requirements, and mask mandates for election workers.
  • Document No. 38: Early Voting
    • Early voting continues to rise, with over 7.9 million early votes cast in the 2022 federal election (51.34% of total votes), up from 6.15 million (40.79%) in 2019. In-person early voting accounted for over 36% of all votes. Postal votes significantly increased to 2.39 million (from 1.29 million in 2019). Mobile polling services were reduced due to the pandemic.
  • Document No. 39: Postal Voting
    • The AEC expresses confidence in the integrity of postal voting processes, which are governed by the Commonwealth Electoral Act 1918. Legislative changes in 2021 modernized postal voting envelopes and allowed for the early extraction of ballot papers from declaration envelopes (from the Monday prior to election day) to speed up counting. In 2022, the AEC issued 2.73 million postal votes (an 87% return rate), with 79.2% of rejected postal votes due to missing elector or witness signatures.
  • Document No. 40: Mobile Polling – 2022 Federal Election
    • Mobile polling services were provided to remote areas, homeless shelters, a limited number of residential aged care facilities (RACFs), and some prisons. The decision to limit services to RACFs was based on health advice and risk assessments, requiring explicit agreement from facilities and adherence to strict COVID-19 safety measures.
  • Document No. 43.2: Overseas voting – 2022 Federal Election
    • COVID-19 and security concerns necessitated adjustments to overseas voting for the 2022 federal election. In-person voting was available at 19 overseas posts, while others relied on postal voting, supported by courier delivery to improve speed and reliability, and diplomatic mail for returns.
  • Document No. 44.2: Senate counting and assurance
    • The AEC uses ballot paper scanning to capture Senate preferences for timely scrutiny. In the 2022 federal election, over 108 million preferences from more than 15.5 million ballot papers were scanned, data-entered, and verified in approximately four weeks. The process involves human verification, robust controls, and security assessments (including IRAP). An external company performs legislated on-site assurance (0.45% exception rate in 2022). Scrutineers can observe the process. The AEC confirmed it has not used Scytl software for any federal election.
  • Document No. 45: EasyCount – Senate
    • "EasyCount – Senate" (ECS) is the AEC's software for distributing preferences in Senate elections. It is rigorously tested, secure, and enables reporting and auditing of results. New legislation will require an external assessor to verify ECS accuracy before the next federal election. The source code is not publicly released due to its commercial use for other elections.
  • Document No. 46: Polling Place Technology – ECLs
    • Investment in polling place technology will increase the number of Electronic Certified Lists (ECLs) from 5,500 (used in 2022) to 10,000 for the next federal election. A new digital tool for polling place Officers in Charge is also being developed to enhance polling place management, integrate with the AEC Command Centre, and improve issue resolution, replacing current paper-based returns.
  • Document No. 47: Voter verification
    • The AEC states that voter verification (requiring identification when casting a vote) is a matter for Parliament and would necessitate legislative change. The AEC warns of potential operational impacts, including increased queue times, higher costs for technology, materials, training, and staff, more time-consuming declaration votes, and the risk of disenfranchising eligible voters.
  • Document No. 48: Candidate qualification checklist
    • The AEC is required to check that mandatory questions on the self-assessment qualification checklist are answered by candidates but does not determine the accuracy of the answers or candidates' eligibility, as this is a matter for the High Court. For the 2022 federal election, 1,624 checklists (totalling 17,117 pages) were published soon after nominations were declared.
  • Document No. 49: Commonwealth Procurement Rules – Environmental sustainability
    • The AEC procures goods and services in line with Commonwealth Procurement Rules, which now explicitly require considering climate change impacts in value-for-money assessments. The AEC's practices include evaluating suppliers' environmental credentials, using recycled content, and recycling/donating end-of-life products. The document notes the challenge of measuring specific climate impacts for small items and balancing this with operational demands.
  • Document No. 50: Cowper Count – 2022 Federal Election
    • An administrative error occurred in the Cowper counting centre during the 2022 federal election, where some House of Representatives ballot papers were inadvertently combined across different pre-poll voting centre counts. The AEC confirmed this error did not impact the overall election results for the division and that all ballot papers were accounted for.
  • Document No. 51: Call Centre worker recordings
    • Addresses social media allegations that AEC call centre staff provided false voting information during the election. The AEC stated it could not verify the authenticity of such isolated claims without reviewing hundreds of thousands of enquiries and asserted that its 105,000 temporary staff are trained and provided with accurate scripts.
  • Document No. 61: Referendums: YES/NO pamphlet and information campaigns
    • No substantive content was provided in the released pages for this document.
  • Document No. 62: Election time public information campaign
    • The AEC conducted a large-scale, multi-channel communication campaign for the 2022 federal election (with a $19 million media placement spend). The campaign aimed to inform and encourage voter participation, covering enrolment deadlines, early voting options, and how to correctly complete ballot papers. It also included "Stop and Consider" messaging against disinformation and information on COVID-19 safety measures, with content translated into 52 languages.
  • Document No. 68: Gilmore Count
    • The AEC denied requests for a recount in the division of Gilmore following the 2022 federal election, made by the Liberal Party candidate. The Divisional Returning Officer and the Electoral Commissioner concluded that the scrutiny process was properly conducted and the margin of 373 votes did not warrant a recount, consistent with AEC policy (automatic recounts occur for margins less than 100 votes). No request was made to the Court of Disputed Returns.
  • Document No. 69: 2GB Discarded ballot papers found – Port Macquarie
    • The AEC is investigating reports of discarded completed ballot papers found in a bin in Port Macquarie. Initial findings suggest these were likely declaration ballot papers that were not returned correctly by voters. The small number of papers indicates no impact on the election result, and no evidence points to an operational issue by the AEC.

Question: In light of the negative comments regarding postal ballots at elections overseas, what 
does the AEC do to ensure the integrity of postal voting in Federal Elections? 

•  Postal voting has been an integral part of Australian elections since before Federation and like 

all AEC processes is strictly governed by legislation and AEC policies and procedures. 

•  The procedures for postal voting are governed by Part XV of the Commonwealth Electoral Act 

1918 (the Electoral Act). 

•  Every postal vote returned to the AEC must be matched to an elector before it is included in 

the count. 

•  The matching process includes the following preliminary scrutiny: 

▪  Name and address match 
▪  Postal vote signed by elector 
▪  Witness requirements 
▪  Check that the elector and witness declarations are dated on or before polling day 
▪  Checks of signatures if deemed necessary 
▪ 

If the elector has already cast a declaration vote that has been counted (whether that be 
by postal, absent or provisional) the vote is not included in the count. 

•  To ensure the secrecy of the ballot, there is a specific process whereby postal votes that are to 
be included into the count have their ballot papers removed with the elector details facing 
down, and the ballot papers are then placed in a ballot box and counted separately in batches. 
Like all processes regarding the counting of ballot papers, these processes can be observed 
by scrutineers. 

• 

Question: Did the increase in postal votes slow down the count and make it longer for seats to be 
declared? 

There are a number of factors that impact the speed of the count and the AECs ability to declare 
the poll in each division. One of these factors is the requirement to wait until 13 days after election 
day to receive returned postal votes, however the 2022 event saw other factors, including the 
increase in the number of candidates and voters marking a first preference for candidates other 
than the two-candidate preferred candidates, also affect the AEC’s ability to declare. 

New legislation that allowed ballots to be extracted from PVCs from the Monday before polling day 
did assist to increase the speed of the count, as did the changes to allow the unfolding and initial 
sorting of ordinary votes taken at pre-poll voting centres from 4pm on polling day. Further 
legislative change, such as beginning these processes earlier and streamlining declaration voting 
may further allow the AEC to provide earlier indicative results in line with public expectations. 

•  While the Electoral Act requires electors to cast their postal vote before 6pm on polling day, 
the AEC is required to wait until 13 days after election day to receive returned postal votes. 
•  A seat cannot be declared until the number of potential votes yet to be counted (which until the 

13th day includes all postal votes issued) cannot change the outcome. 

•  This can mean that votes that may decide the election may not be received until almost 
2 weeks after polling day. Therefore, if there is a greater percentage of postal votes, the 
likelihood of these votes delaying declaration is higher (particularly in marginal or close seats). 

•  The AEC’s integrity process for the return of postal votes also means that there are more 

checks and balances required than counting ordinary ballot papers cast in a physical polling 
place. This too can slow down the count and is resource intensive. The AEC applies the same 
integrity processes to all returned postal votes, regardless of whether the result of a seat or the 
election is beyond doubt.

Position 

The scanning of Senate ballot papers, capture of preferences and the software

After legislative change prior to the 2016 federal election, the AEC determined that 
the scanning of ballot papers to capture preferences was the only method 
available to ensure that the Senate scrutinies were completed within required 
timeframes. The solution significantly reduces the human effort, time and cost of 
capturing voter preferences in a full manual construct. There are robust controls 
and validations to ensure the continued integrity of the elections. 

Points 

• Prior to every election the AEC undertakes extensive rigorous testing, security
assessments and assurance through internal and external specialists of the
software and processes.

• At the 2022 federal election more than 108 million preferences from over
15.5 million ballot papers were scanned, data entered, and verified in
approximately four weeks after polling day.

• To ensure accuracy, all preferences are data entered by at least one human
operator and compared to captured (scanned) preferences. All interpretation
discrepancies are escalated for further action.

• The AEC is has implemented, and complies with, all components of the

Electoral Legislation Amendment (Assurance of Senate Counting) Act 2021.

• The AEC demonstrates the software used to support the scrutiny for each

Senate election is secure through security risk assessments which includes
AEC and provider systems.

• The AEC engaged an external company to complete the legislated on-site
assurance process during the Senate scrutiny (s273AC). This external
company oversaw the selection and inspection of ballot papers to demonstrate
the preferences have been correctly captured within AEC systems. This
process was open to inspection by scrutineers. Nationally, the identified
exception rate for the 2022 federal election was 0.45%.

• Scrutineers can monitor all processing of Senate ballot papers at Central

Senate Scrutiny centres in each state and territory.

FOUND – PORT MACQUARIE  

Commissioner’s summary 

Issue 

2GB Ben Fordham Breakfast Show aired/published a story on 22 June 2022 
suggesting a listener had found completed ballot papers in a bin in Port 
Macquarie. 

Position 

The AEC upholds electoral integrity and public trust in electoral processes and 
has strong ballot paper handling policies that all staff are trained to adhere to. 

Points 

• The AEC’s investigation into the discarded ballot papers is ongoing.

• At this stage the finding is that the completed ballot papers are likely to have
been discarded declaration ballot papers (that is, an out-of-area voter should
have returned their completed ballot paper to be placed inside a completed
declaration envelope, but in these instances, they have likely not been
returned as per the instruction).

•

Initial indications are that the ballot papers are likely informal, and due to the
small number, there is no impact on the result of any electoral division.

• There is no evidence regarding their origin that points to an operational issue

impacting the election.

Talking points 

Question: Why were completed ballot papers found in a bin in Port Macquarie? 

• The AEC’s investigation into the discarded ballot papers is ongoing and a report will be written

once finalised.

• At this stage the finding is that the completed ballot papers are likely to have been discarded
declaration ballot papers (that is, an out-of-area voter should have returned their completed
ballot paper to be placed inside a completed declaration envelope, but in these instances they
have likely not been returned as per the instruction).

• Ballot papers in the AEC’s possession (prior to issuing to a voter in-person or via post, or after

receipt of completed ballots from a voter) are tracked from point to point with a range of
security measures in place.

• Ballot papers in a voter’s possession (e.g. when voting via post or in-person) are the

responsibility of the voter themselves to return to the AEC in the required timeframe in order to
be counted.
