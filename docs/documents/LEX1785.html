FOI Request LEX1785

Date: 2022

This FOI request (No. LEX1785) sought the application made by Mr. Max Kaye under section 141(2) of the Commonwealth Electoral Act 1918 on 14 April 2022 to the Electoral Commission.

The single document released in response is "Application made under section 141(2) of the Commonwealth Electoral Act 1918 by Mr. Max Kaye", dated 14 April 2022.

The main content of the document, focusing on the FOI request, includes:

  • Purpose of the Application: Mr. Max Kaye (a founder and former officer of The Flux Party) requests the Australian Electoral Commission (AEC) to review its decision, made on 24 March 2022, to deregister "VOTEFLUX.ORG | Upgrade Democracy!" (Flux) under s137(6) of the Electoral Act.
  • Core Argument for Review: Kaye contends that the AEC uses a "faulty method to test party eligibility with regards to sufficient membership" and provides detailed statistical analysis as grounds for this claim. He states he is "dissatisfied with the decision to deregister Flux" due to this flawed methodology.
  • Critique of AEC's Methodology:
    • Hypothetical Scenario: Kaye presents a hypothetical eligible party (2,700 legitimate members out of 9,000 total, 4,500 validatable). Despite this eligibility, his calculations show that such a party would have an extremely low probability of passing the AEC's test (0.0020% ± 0.0012% chance of success), implying a false negative rate exceeding 99.99%.
    • Application to Flux: He states that the parameters of the hypothetical case are "spitting distance" from Flux's second membership test (Feb/March 2022). For Flux specifically, the test results in a false negative probability of 0.999978 ± 0.000013.
    • Sampling Bias: During Flux's second test, the AEC sampled only the "top 1,650 names" from Flux's alphabetically sorted list of 4,680 members. This meant only members whose first names started with A through G were sampled, which Kaye argues further biases the test if membership isn't evenly distributed alphabetically.
    • Limited Sample Size: The AEC's policy limits submitted membership lists to 1,650 names (previously 550), regardless of a party's actual, larger membership. This artificial limit significantly compromises the accuracy of the test and means a party with thousands of eligible members can still fail.
    • Definition of "Rigged" and "Farce": Kaye explicitly defines the AEC's method as "rigged" (outcome pre-arranged/fixed, unfair) and a "farce" (unfair, empty act, done for show with a known outcome) because of its high false negative rate for eligible parties.
  • Analysis of Flux's Tests:
    • Flux failed its 2021 test, despite having "at least 4680 members whose details have been matched against the electoral roll." Kaye's analysis showed a 71.7% chance of a false negative for this test.
    • He argues that the AEC's method can become less reliable as a party gains members, and in some extreme (yet realistic) scenarios, it can fail 100% of the time for an eligible party.
  • Critique of AEC's "Reasons" for Deregistration: Kaye asserts that the AEC's statement of reasons for deregistering Flux ignored his arguments, dismissed the statistical analysis, and contradictorily relied on the very method he criticized, stating it was "rational, fair and practical."
  • Systemic Implications: Kaye argues that the AEC's policies, particularly the limits on membership list submissions, have "systemically disadvantaged non-parliamentary parties" and made the AEC "complicit" in entrenching the power of political elites by making it harder for new or smaller parties to remain registered.
  • Supporting Data: The document includes detailed statistical graphs (Probability Mass Functions) and tables to illustrate the probabilities of false negatives under various conditions, alongside historical examples of other "suspected farces" (past deregistration/refusal cases with high false negative probabilities). It also highlights a persistent typo in historical AEC testing tables that was enforced.

2.	Regular	Membership	Testing

Every	few	years,	the	Australian	Electoral	Commission	(AEC)	will	check	that	each	political	party	has

enough	members	according	to	the	legislative	requirement.	The	party	must	provide	a	list	of	1500	to
1650	names	(inclusive)	to	use	as	evidence	of	their	eligibility.	The	AEC	will	then	filter	out	some	names

(duplicates,	deceased	members,	etc).	That	produces	a	NEW	list	of	≤	1650	names.	Then,	the	AEC	will

do	a	statistical	sampling	of	members	and	will	use	that	to	determine	whether	a	party	is	eligible.

Particularly,	a	small	subset	of	members	are	selected	and	contacted,	asking	for	a	yes/no	confirmation

of	membership.	Non-responses	are	skipped.	A	“No”	answer	counts	as	a	failure	–	this	is	a	membership

denial.	In	this	document	and	associated	code:	“failure	rate”	refers	to	the	rate	at	which	members

respond	“No”.

The	AEC	does	not	accept	lists	larger	than	1650;	there	is	no	chance	for	a	party	to	replace	any	of	those
filtered	members;	that	filtering	process	increases	the	chance	of	false	negatives	(when	list	length	is

limited	+	excluding	duplicates);	parties	are	not	told	which	members	were	filtered	(even	those	which

are	deceased)	so	they	cannot	be	proactively	removed;	and,	finally,	the	standard	of	statistical

evaluation	is	to	assume	that	the	list	of	1650	members	were	the	only	members	of	the	party.	Zero

consideration	is	given	beyond	this,	outside	the	chance	to	respond	–	a	tactic	that	has,	historically,

performed	poorly	except	by	the	grace	of	the	AEC.	How	many	parties	have	been	wrongly	denied

registration	due	to	this	artificial	limit?	Nobody	knows.

members	that	a	list	of	1650	members	is	randomly	sampled	from.

excluding	those	that	we	could	not	validate.	The	AEC	is	sometimes	able	to	validate	members	that	we

cannot,	and	we	have	at	least	4285	additional	members	that	we	could	contact	with	a	request	for
