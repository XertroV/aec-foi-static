FOI Request LEX7512

Date: 2025

This FOI request centers on the legal and constitutional status of Norfolk Island and its indigenous inhabitants concerning their obligation to participate in Australian federal elections.

Main Purpose of the FOI Request

The primary purpose of the FOI request, initiated by the Norfolk Island Chamber of Commerce (NICC) and its President, Cherri Buffett, is to challenge the Australian Electoral Commission's (AEC) requirement for indigenous Norfolk Islanders to enrol and vote in Australian federal elections, including the upcoming 2025 election. The NICC asserts that Norfolk Island is not legally part of the Commonwealth of Australia, that indigenous Norfolk Islanders are not Australian citizens, and therefore, the Australian Government has no authority or administration rights over the island or its people. The request seeks specific legal and lawful documentation to substantiate Australia's claim over Norfolk Island.

Documents from the FOI Request

The documents released and referenced in this FOI request package include:
* Initial Correspondence: Emails from the Norfolk Island Chamber of Commerce (Doc 1, 1G, 1H, 11, 11A) and Cherri Buffett (Doc 2, 3, 4) to the AEC, copying various Australian government officials and parliamentarians.
* AEC Responses: Emails from the AEC's Chief Legal Officer, Andrew Johnson, and Acting Electoral Commissioner, Jeff Pope (Doc 2, 4A, 6, 7, 11, 11B, 12).
* Internal Government Briefs: Attachments from the Department of Infrastructure, Regional Development and Cities concerning the "Preamble to the Norfolk Island Act 1979" (Doc 1A) and a "Petition to the United Nations (UN)" (Doc 1B).
* Historical Documents: Copies of a Queen Victoria Royal Warrant (Dec 1856) (Doc 1F) and various historical Orders in Council, letters, and Hansard extracts (Doc 1D, 1E, 1G, 1H) provided by the Chamber to support their claims.
* Refused Document: One document (Doc 13) related to a meeting with the Australian Government Solicitor regarding joint candidates was refused in full.

Main Content from the FOI Request Documents

Arguments presented by the Norfolk Island Chamber of Commerce (NICC) and Cherri Buffett:
* Norfolk Island's Legal Status: The Chamber maintains that Norfolk Island is a "distinct and separate Colony," established for the Pitcairn Settlers and their descendants by Queen Victoria's 1856 Order in Council and Royal Warrant, and has never been legally annexed or transferred to Australia.
* Citizenship and Electoral Rights: They argue that indigenous Norfolk Islanders are not Australian citizens and, therefore, are not subject to Australian electoral laws.
* Invalidity of Australian Legislation:
* The Norfolk Island Act 1913 and subsequent Australian legislation are deemed invalid because necessary Imperial legislation for annexation or transfer from the United Kingdom is missing.
* They assert that Section 122 of the Australian Constitution ("Government of territories") was not properly followed, as Norfolk Island was never "placed" under Commonwealth authority by the Sovereign before being "accepted."
* They highlight that Norfolk Island was never part of New South Wales, citing the NSW Constitution Act 1902 boundaries.
* They invoke the Statute of Westminster Adoption Act 1942, arguing its sections prevent the Commonwealth from legislating local government laws for Norfolk Island.
* Supporting Evidence:
* Quotes from various Australian government documents (including Hansard 1901, a 1971 confidential report, and a 1999 Royal Australian Mint statement) are provided, allegedly stating that Norfolk Island is not legally part of the Commonwealth, has never been ceded or annexed, and that no land transfer instruments exist.
* A previous FOI request to the Governor-General's office reportedly found no documents for the transfer of land interest or original claim of right over Norfolk Island.
* They specifically cite Justice Callinan's comments in Bennett v Commonwealth (2007) that Norfolk Island "did not on its acceptance by Australia, nor has it since, become part of Australia, geographically or politically," arguing this challenges the premise of Australia's control.
* Requested Documents: The Chamber explicitly requests certified copies of:
1. Documents proving Norfolk Island was annexed to New South Wales.
2. Results of a New South Wales referendum agreeing to this annexation.
3. Documents showing Norfolk Island was then annexed and handed over from NSW to the Commonwealth.
4. The Imperial Legislation required for Norfolk Island to become part of the Commonwealth.

Response from the Australian Electoral Commission (AEC):
* Statutory Obligation: The AEC states it is a statutory authority legally required to administer federal election events in accordance with the Commonwealth Electoral Act 1918 and the Referendum (Machinery Provisions) Act 1984, both of which explicitly extend to Norfolk Island.
* Compulsory Enrolment and Voting: The AEC maintains it is compulsory for all eligible Australian citizens residing on Norfolk Island to enrol and vote, with penalties for non-compliance.
* High Court Rulings: The AEC relies on High Court decisions, specifically Berwick Ltd v Gray (1976) and Bennett v Commonwealth (2007), which it asserts confirm that Commonwealth laws can apply to Norfolk Island and that granting electoral rights to Norfolk Islanders is a valid exercise of Parliament's power under Section 122 of the Constitution. The AEC explicitly refutes the Chamber's interpretation of Justice Callinan's judgment in Bennett, stating he rejected the plaintiffs' submissions.
* Jurisdiction: The AEC declines to engage further on constitutional matters, advising the Chamber to direct these concerns to the Department of Infrastructure, Transport, Regional Development, Communications and the Arts (responsible for territories), the Attorney-General’s Department (constitutional matters), or the Department of Home Affairs (citizenship).
* Document Holding: The AEC states it does not hold the specific historical documents requested by the Chamber regarding annexation or transfer of Norfolk Island's status.

In April 2016, Geoffrey Robertson QC, acting on behalf of the Norfolk Island People for 
Democracy (NIPD), submitted a petition to the Chair of the UN General Assembly’s 
Fourth Committee (the Special Political and Decolonisation Committee) requesting 
Norfolk Island be inscribed on the UN’s list of non-self-governing territories.  

The UN list of non-self-governing territories identifies territories that the UN General 
Assembly deems to be "non-self-governing" and subject to a decolonisation process.  

The UN has not scheduled time to consider the petition.  

Advice from the Attorney-General’s Department is that Norfolk Island does not meet the 
UN’s criteria for inscription as a non-self-governing territory.  

People from Norfolk Island have previously petitioned the UN for recognition as a 
non-self-governing territory (in 1977 and 1994) without success.  

On 8 June 2018, the Council of Elders announced in the Norfolk Islander newspaper that it 
has contracted ‘Expert Advisory Services’ to carry out a Self-Governance Assessment of 
Norfolk Island and to draft a UN General Assembly resolution to expedite the review of 
their petition. This work was expected to be completed by the end of August 2018 but the 
Department has not had any information to indicate this has occurred.  

Background: 

NIPD 

 

 

NIPD was established in June 2015 with the aim of having Norfolk Island inscribed on the 
UN’s list of non-self-governing territories.  

It is difficult to gauge the true level of support for NIPD on Norfolk Island. The group 
claim to have over 1,000 supporters (likely to include visitors to the Island) and to be 
representing the majority of views. NIPD’s fundraising methods and affiliations, however, 
have been the subject of some concern and scrutiny in public forums.  

Process for Inscription 

 

 

The UN General Assembly must approve any addition of a territory to the list of non-self-
governing territories. This can follow a recommendation made by the Special Committee 
on Decolonisation (also known as the Committee of 24, or C24) which monitors and 
reviews the list of non-self-governing territories. 

Alternatively, a draft resolution may be proposed before the UN General Assembly by a 
Member State. NIPD would need to obtain the sponsorship of a Member State to the UN to 
put forward the draft resolution to the UN General Assembly plenary.

Document Summary

The document reveals an ongoing dispute regarding Norfolk Island's legal status and its inclusion in Australian Federal elections.

Their arguments, supported by extensive historical documents and legal interpretations within the attachments, assert:

    • Norfolk Island was established as a "distinct and separate Settlement" by Queen Victoria in 1856 for Pitcairn settlers, with its own self-governance.
    • The 1913 Norfolk Island Act, which purportedly "accepted" the island into the Commonwealth, is invalid as it did not follow the proper constitutional process (Section 122, requiring the Queen to "place" and the Commonwealth to "accept").
    • Historical Australian government documents and a 1999 Royal Australian Mint letter (cited in the email chain and attachments) acknowledge Norfolk Island was "never" under NSW government, not legally part of the Commonwealth, and "never been ceded or annexed to Australia."
