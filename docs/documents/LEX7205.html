FOI Request LEX7205

Date: 2025

Summary of FOI Request Documents: Engagement with Online Platforms on Electoral Integrity

Main Purpose of the FOI Request

The FOI request primarily seeks information regarding the Australian Electoral Commission's (AEC) interactions, meetings, and communications with major online platforms (Google/YouTube, Meta/Facebook/Instagram, Microsoft/LinkedIn, TikTok) concerning electoral integrity, the spread of misinformation/disinformation, the impact of AI-generated content, and preparations for the upcoming Australian Federal Election in 2025 (FE25). The focus is on how these platforms are collaborating with the AEC's Electoral Integrity Assurance Taskforce (EIAT) and Defending Democracy Unit (DDU) to safeguard democratic processes.

Documents from the FOI Request

The released documents include:
* Meeting Invitations and Confirmations: Numerous emails scheduling and confirming meetings between AEC/EIAT representatives and the online platforms.
* Email Correspondence: Extensive exchanges discussing meeting agendas, follow-up actions, sharing insights, and requesting information.
* Formal Letters: Correspondence between the AEC Acting Electoral Commissioner and the CEOs/Directors of the tech companies regarding commitments to electoral integrity accords.
* Internal Briefs and Attachments: Documents like Microsoft's "Best Practices for combating abusive media and deepfakes" and letters outlining company policies and initiatives.
* Redacted Documents: Several documents and subject lines were withheld or partially accessed due to exemptions (e.g., S22, S37, S47E(d), S47F(1), S47G).

Main Content from the FOI Request Documents

Overarching Themes and Commitments:
* Preparation for FE25: All platforms are actively engaging with the AEC and EIAT to prepare for the federal election, focusing on the information environment.
* Electoral Integrity: A central theme, encompassing efforts to counter misinformation, disinformation, cyber threats, and foreign interference.
* AI-Generated Content (AIGC) & Deepfakes: A significant new area of focus, with discussions on detection methods, labeling policies, content provenance, and the potential for misuse. Platforms are exploring and implementing features like watermarking (e.g., Google's SynthID, Microsoft's Content Credentials).
* Lessons from Global Elections 2024: Platforms share observations and insights from elections in other countries (e.g., US, India, UK, EU, Taiwan, Indonesia) and how these learnings are being applied to Australia.
* Referral & Escalation Pathways: Confirmation and streamlining of mechanisms for the AEC/EIAT to report problematic content or issues directly to the platforms.
* Media Literacy & Education Campaigns: Platforms are engaged in or discussing campaigns to help voters identify trusted information sources and critical thinking.
* Security for Electoral Processes: Discussions include protecting campaigns, election officials, and critical infrastructure from cyber threats.

Specific Content by Platform:

Google/YouTube:
* Meeting Agendas: Discussions revolve around Google's approach to AI in elections, content provenance on YouTube, addressing Sovereign Citizen movement misinformation, and overall support for FE25.
* AI & Misinformation: Google has restricted election-related queries on Gemini, implemented ads disclosures for synthetic content in election ads, and YouTube requires creators to disclose realistic altered/synthetic content with labels.
* Platform Tools: YouTube's Community Guidelines, AI labelling, and the "Hit Pause" media literacy campaign are highlighted.
* Incident Response: Prompt action on reported videos (e.g., termination of a channel spreading false claims about digital ID for FE25).
* Tech Accord: Google reaffirms its commitment to the "Tech Accord to Combat Deceptive Use of AI in 2024 Elections," detailing steps such as Model Cards, AI Prohibited Use Policy, AI-Assisted Red Teaming, and expanding SynthID watermarking.

Meta (Facebook/Instagram):
* Media Literacy: Meta supported an "AAP Check the Facts Media Literacy campaign" for the 2023 referendum and a new campaign in H2 2024 focused on AI-generated content awareness.
* Coordinated Inauthentic Behaviour (CIB): Details provided on network disruption efforts, including against the Russian 'Doppelganger' group, and reporting in Meta's Transparency Report.
* AI Policy: Changes to manipulated media policy include adding "AI info" labels to a wider range of content and requiring disclosure for AI/digital methods in political advertising.
* Global Observations: Meta shares observations from 2024 global elections (US, India, Indonesia, EU) regarding AI's impact, freedom of expression, and foreign interference.
* Escalation: Ongoing confirmation of pathways for EIAT/AEC to flag issues.

Microsoft/LinkedIn:
* Democracy Forward Program: Microsoft's "Democracy Forward" team offers in-person briefings and training on safeguarding democratic processes, promoting a healthy information ecosystem, and corporate civic responsibility.
* Cybersecurity & Threat Intelligence: Insights from Microsoft Threat Analysis Centre (MTAC) and Microsoft Threat Intelligence Centre (MSTIC) on nation-state threats, cybercriminal activity, and influence operations.
* AI & Content Integrity: Implementation of Content Credentials (C2PA standard) across products (Bing Image Creator, Microsoft Designer, Copilot, Azure OpenAI DALL-E) to mark AI-generated images with indicators of origin.
* Tools & Services: Offers AccountGuard (no-cost cybersecurity for high-risk users), Azure for Election service (security reviews for critical systems), and Content Integrity Tools for political campaigns/election authorities to apply verifiable signatures to authentic content.
* AEC Employee Protection: AEC requested to block Microsoft AI image generation of specific prominent AEC employees and federal candidates.
* LinkedIn Collaboration: Discussions to establish referral pathways and engagement for election integrity concerns.
* Public Awareness: Development of "Check, Recheck, Vote" website and "Real or Not?" quiz to educate voters on deepfakes.

TikTok:
* Policy Updates: Updated Civic and Election Integrity policies, including protections for election workers and refined AIGC policies requiring user labeling and auto-labeling. Content sharing fake authoritative sources or bullying public figures is prohibited.
* State-Affiliated Media: Expanded policies to make state-affiliated media accounts ineligible for recommendations outside their home country and monthly public reporting on Covert Influence Operations.
* FE25 Preparations: Establishment of a dedicated core working group, prohibition on paid political advertising, active reporting portal access for AEC, and in-app redirection to authoritative election information (AEC's website and TikTok account).
* Proactive Measures: Conducting impersonation sweeps for verified political candidate accounts and issuing policy guidance specific to the election for moderation systems.
* AEC Questions: AEC queries focus on positive uses of TikTok in elections, algorithm priority for authoritative sources, election-specific filters, and changes to referral pathways due to AI-enabled misinformation.

This collaboration is framed around ensuring "electoral integrity" and combating "misinformation" and "disinformation" ahead of the upcoming Australian federal election.

Social Justice:
The government's stated aims to safeguard the electoral process and ensure access to "timely and accurate information" for voters generally align with progressive values, as informed participation is foundational to a just democracy. Concerns are raised by the AEC regarding content that risks "disenfranchising Australian voters," such as false claims about digital ID requirements for voting. The proactive efforts to protect electoral workers from "harassment and intimidation" also resonate with social justice principles, acknowledging the vital role these individuals play in upholding democratic rights.

Environmental Impact:
These documents do not contain any information directly related to environmental impact or climate change.

Wealth Distribution and Corporate Influence:
A significant theme is the government's profound reliance on private, for-profit tech companies to manage the digital information environment crucial to democratic functioning. This raises serious concerns from a left-leaning perspective, as it implies a transfer of power from public institutions to private corporations whose primary fiduciary duty is to shareholders, not the public good. While tech companies promote their "corporate civic responsibility" and tools like "Democracy Forward," these initiatives are ultimately integrated into their commercial strategies. The fact that the AEC must formally request and negotiate access to information, content moderation policies, and even the ability to "block AI image generation" of its employees highlights the corporations' gatekeeping power. A notable alignment with progressive values is TikTok's explicit "prohibition on paid political advertising," which aims to limit the influence of wealth in elections, contrasting with Google/YouTube's focus on "ads disclosures" for synthetic content.

Civil Liberties:
The focus on combating "misinformation" and "disinformation" presents a double-edged sword for civil liberties. While crucial for a fair election, it opens the door to potential overreach in content moderation. The documents show the AEC directly requesting Microsoft to "block AI image generation of specific AEC employees," including those who appeared in public AEC videos. This type of intervention, extending beyond security concerns to image control, signals a troubling expansion of government requests into expressive liberties, potentially setting a precedent for suppressing legitimate, if critical, digital expression.

Furthermore, a proposed amendment to electoral legislation, which would make it an offense to video or audio record in polling places and post it online without permission, is a significant deviation from civil liberties principles. This directly impacts freedom of the press and the public's right to observe and document democratic processes, eroding transparency.

Finally, the extensive redactions throughout the documents (using exemptions like S22, S47E(d), S47F(1), S47G, and particularly S37 for "Refuse Access" documents) severely limit public understanding of the specifics of these interactions. This lack of transparency undermines the public's right to know how its government is engaging with powerful corporations on critical democratic issues, fostering an environment where corporate influence could operate with reduced public scrutiny.

Addressing Deepfake Creation

origin. One way to accomplish this is through content provenance, enabled by an open standard

Preparation and prevention

Preventing the malicious use of deepfake and generative Al technology presents a significant challenge. While
major commercial platforms have imposed usage restrictions, open-source models and alternative platforms
often lack such controls. To address this, initiatives like the Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity
(C2PA), in which Microsoft played an instrumental role, are pioneering technologies enabling creators to
embed digital signatures in media, verifying their authenticity and origin. Microsoft's Content Integrity tool
simplifies credentialing, allowing users to add verifiable signatures to their content. For more information,

contact us at campaignsuccess@microsoft.com

While C2PA doesn't directly prevent the spread of inauthentic content, it enhances preparedness by promoting
verified content, facilitating easier differentiation from abusive or deceptive media. Campaigns can proactively
incorporate content credentialing into their workflows, ensuring all released media contains authentic

signatures.

Additionally, campaigns must stay informed about social media policies and reporting mechanisms for abusive
or deceptive content. Understanding platform nuances is essential for effectively navigating the digital
landscape.

Maintaining credibility is crucial when combating disinformation. Campaigns must uphold integrity and
transparency to preserve their moral standing, as ethical lapses can undermine their ability to confront attacks
convincingly. Upholding ethical practices is paramount for campaigns seeking credibility in the face of

disinformation.
