FOI Request LEX3070

Date: 2022

Summary of FOI Request Documents (LEX3070)

Main Purpose of the FOI Request

The main purpose of the Freedom of Information (FOI) request (LEX3070) was to obtain "all applications made to the Australian Electoral Commission (AEC) under s 141 (2) of the Commonwealth Electoral Act 1918 during the year 2022 prior to 21st June 2022." Section 141(2) of the Act pertains to requests for review of decisions made by the Electoral Commission, typically concerning the registration or deregistration of political parties.

Documents from the FOI Request

The released documents include:

  • A "Schedule of Published Documents" outlining nine items related to the FOI request.
  • Various communications, primarily emails and letters, from political parties or individuals to the AEC. These communications mostly constitute formal requests for review of AEC decisions under s 141(2) or related objections.
  • One detailed statistical analysis report, "AEC Party Membership Test Methodology is Rigged! A Statistical Analysis of AEC Methodology and Graphs (of PMFs)," submitted as part of a review request by the Flux party.
  • Standard redaction codes outlining reasons for information withholding (e.g., personal information, business information, legal professional communication).

Main Content from FOI Request Documents

The core content of the released documents revolves around challenges to the Australian Electoral Commission's decisions regarding party registration and deregistration, primarily focusing on the methodology for assessing party membership.

1. Challenges to AEC's Membership Testing Methodology (Common Theme):

Several parties, including the Democratic Labour Party (DLP), VOTEFLUX.ORG | Upgrade Democracy! (Flux), and the Health Australia Party (HAP), submitted requests for review of their deregistration, predominantly arguing that the AEC's statistical methodology for verifying party membership is flawed and unfair.

  • Arguments raised by DLP, Flux, and HAP:
    • Statistical Validity: They contend that the AEC's sampling method (e.g., testing only a small sample of members) is unreliable and does not definitively prove a party's membership numbers. Flux provided extensive statistical analysis by Max Kaye, arguing that the methodology has a high "false negative" rate (over 99.99% for Flux in a hypothetical scenario where it met the membership threshold) and that its outcomes are "predetermined" and "rigged."
    • Membership List Limits: Parties criticized the AEC's policy of limiting the number of members a party can submit for verification (e.g., 1650 members), arguing it prevents them from demonstrating their true membership numbers (e.g., Flux claimed 4,680 members, HAP claimed 3,000-4,000 members). They argue this policy is not legislated and unduly disadvantages parties.
    • Procedural Fairness: Claims of denial of natural justice were made, including:
      • AEC ignoring requests to submit additional member lists while allegedly permitting other parties to do so without explicit permission (DLP).
      • AEC's selective sampling (e.g., Flux alleged the AEC sampled only from the top 1,650 names alphabetically, excluding many members).
      • Denial of access to comprehensive electoral roll databases (ELIAS) for parties to accurately prune their member lists (HAP).
      • Allegations of "apprehended bias" against certain party types (DLP).
  • Consequences and Historical Context: The statistical analysis by Max Kaye further suggests that the AEC's methodology, combined with changes in electoral law (e.g., increasing required members from 500 to 1500), has "systemically disadvantaged non-parliamentary parties" and acted as a tool for "eliminating competition." It identifies several historical cases as "suspected" or "possible farces" where the AEC's testing method likely had a very low accuracy rate.

2. Suspected Clerical Error (Australian Progressives):

  • The Australian Progressives requested an urgent internal review of their deregistration, alleging a "clerical error" in the AEC's assessment of their membership. They noted a drastic and inexplicable drop in their membership match rate between two submitted lists (from 96% to 12%), which they believe was not justified by amendments made to the list.

3. Objection to Party Registration (David Pocock Party):

  • An individual requested an internal review of the AEC's decision to register the David Pocock party. The objections were based on two main grounds:
    • Candidate Eligibility: David Pocock's public admission of foreign (Zimbabwean) citizenship, arguing that registering a party named after an individual ineligible for election is contrary to the Commonwealth Electoral Act's purpose and could mislead the public.
    • Party Name Consistency: The name "David Pocock" was deemed inconsistent with the legal concept of a "party," which should represent a collective rather than an individual. Concerns were raised about potential confusion on ballot papers and an unfair advantage for the candidate.

4. Objection to Party Logo (Australian Federation Party):

  • An individual objected to the proposed logo for the Australian Federation Party. The concern was that a "tick" symbol within the logo could be misinterpreted by voters as a mark for the ballot box, potentially leading to invalidated votes. (Note: While included in the released documents, this is an objection to a logo under s 134(6)(c), not a s 141(2) review of a registration/deregistration decision.)

In summary, the documents reveal a strong theme of challenges to the AEC's administrative processes, particularly its membership verification methodology, with parties arguing that the methods are statistically flawed, procedurally unfair, and lead to arbitrary or predetermined deregistration outcomes.

Every	few	years,	the	Australian	Electoral	Commission	(AEC)	will	check	that	each	political	party	has

enough	members	according	to	the	legislative	requirement.	The	party	must	provide	a	list	of	1500	to

do	a	statistical	sampling	of	members	and	will	use	that	to	determine	whether	a	party	is	eligible.

Particularly,	a	small	subset	of	members	are	selected	and	contacted,	asking	for	a	yes/no	confirmation

of	membership.	Non-responses	are	skipped.	A	“No”	answer	counts	as	a	failure	–	this	is	a	membership

denial.	In	this	document	and	associated	code:	“failure	rate”	refers	to	the	rate	at	which	members

respond	“No”.

The	AEC	does	not	accept	lists	larger	than	1650;	there	is	no	chance	for	a	party	to	replace	any	of	those
filtered	members;	that	filtering	process	increases	the	chance	of	false	negatives	(when	list	length	is

limited	+	excluding	duplicates);	parties	are	not	told	which	members	were	filtered	(even	those	which

are	deceased)	so	they	cannot	be	proactively	removed;	and,	finally,	the	standard	of	statistical

evaluation	is	to	assume	that	the	list	of	1650	members	were	the	only	members	of	the	party.	Zero

consideration	is	given	beyond	this,	outside	the	chance	to	respond	–	a	tactic	that	has,	historically,

performed	poorly	except	by	the	grace	of	the	AEC.	How	many	parties	have	been	wrongly	denied

Excess	Capacity	Explanation

Excess	capacity	here	refers	to	additional	members	that,	if	not	for	the	AEC’s	limit,	a	party	could

provide	–	expressed	as	a	percentage	of	the	limit.	If	a	party	requires	10%	excess	capacity,	and	the

limit	of	a	membership	list	is	1650,	then	that	party	must	be	capable	of	providing	a	list	of	1815

members	that	a	list	of	1650	members	is	randomly	sampled	from.

For	comparison:	in	Flux’s	case,	we	had	3030	additional	members	(excess	capacity	of	184%)	–

excluding	those	that	we	could	not	validate.	The	AEC	is	sometimes	able	to	validate	members	that	we

cannot,	and	we	have	at	least	4285	additional	members	that	we	could	contact	with	a	request	for

them	to	update	their	details.

Issue (2) The proposed name ‘David Pocock’ is not consistent with the concept of a party. 

The reasons for this objection are set out in more detail in the decision-maker’s summary of objections 
from Ernst, Neil and Lis. I adopt all those reasons in this objection-I think it is unnecessary to set them out

decision. 

Again the decision-maker has adopted a ‘ticking boxes’ approach without understanding the scheme and 
purpose of the Act. 

It must surely be obvious that an application to be registered as a party must satisfy the legal concept of 
‘party’. To explain this by analogy, if seeking to register a motor vehicle it is not possible to register a cat, 
because a cat is not a motor vehicle. Similarly, to register a party there must be something that satisfies 
the legal concept of a party. As a matter of common sense, a single person or the name of a person does 
not constitute a party. A party simply put is a body of persons united in some sort of cause or opinion or 
otherwise engaged together. These matters have been set out in greater length in the objections from 
Ernst, Neil and Lis set out in the decision. A party may incorporate the name of a person, so, for example, 
‘David Pocock for Canberra’ would satisfy the party concept. 

The error in the decision can be illustrated by drawing attention to the consequences for the ballot paper.  

If the application were allowed to stand the ballot paper would have David Pocock above the line, as a 
name.  

Electors wishing to vote ‘below the line’ would also find the name David Pocock below the line. 

Surely this outcome would be perverse. Surely the scheme object and purpose of the Act envisage that the 
identification ‘above the line’ must be different from the name that appears below the line.  

The consequential confusion and uncertainty is obvious. Some electors may tick David Pocock boxes both 
above and below the line, some may be confused about how to allocate preferences and so on.  

In short, what appears above the line and what appears below the line must surely be different. A party 
must be different from and more than just a name. 

I add that it is arguable that this outcome would give Mr Pocock an unfair advantage over other
