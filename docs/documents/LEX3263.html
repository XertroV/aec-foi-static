FOI Request LEX3263

Date: 2022

FOI Request Summary: Overseas Voting in New Zealand

Main Purpose of the FOI Request

The Freedom of Information (FOI) request (No. LEX3263) sought specific information regarding Australian federal election voting processes for electors in New Zealand across the last four general elections. The request had four key categories:
* Category A: Number of declaration votes (in-person and postal) cast at the Australian High Commission in Wellington, New Zealand.
* Category B: Number of declaration votes (in-person and postal) cast at the Australian Consulate-General in Auckland, New Zealand.
* Category C: Number of postal votes issued to New Zealand addresses and returned directly to the Australian Electoral Commission (AEC).
* Category D: Any briefing or decision documents related to the decision not to offer in-person voting at the Australian High Commission in Wellington for the 2022 federal election.

Documents from the FOI Request

Two documents were released in response to this FOI request:
1. Document created in response to this FOI request (13 September 2022): This document contains the quantitative data for Categories A, B, and C.
2. DFAT Minute: Australian federal election – 2022 window – Overseas voting risk analysis and recommendations (20 December 2021): This document addresses Category D.

Main Content from the FOI Request Documents

The released documents provide the following key information:

1. Declaration Votes Data (Categories A, B, and C):

  • Category A: Australian High Commission, Wellington, NZ

    • 2022: In-person: 0, Postal: 800
    • 2019: In-person: 667, Postal: 5
    • 2016: In-person: 474, Postal: 9
    • 2013: In-person: 469, Postal: 4
    • Note: In-person voting was not offered at Wellington in 2022, resulting in a significant increase in postal votes returned to this location.

  • Category B: Australian Consulate-General, Auckland, NZ

    • 2022: In-person: 749, Postal: 479
    • 2019: In-person: 967, Postal: 19
    • 2016: In-person: 805, Postal: 20
    • 2013: In-person: 819, Postal: 29
    • Note: Auckland continued to offer in-person voting in 2022, but also saw a substantial increase in postal votes received at the post compared to previous elections.

  • Category C: Postal Votes Returned Directly to AEC from New Zealand Addresses

    • 2022: 360
    • 2019: 1,389
    • 2016: 1,309
    • 2013: Not provided (indicated by '-').
    • Note: There was a significant decrease in postal votes returned directly to the AEC from NZ addresses in 2022 compared to 2019 and 2016.

2. Decision Regarding In-Person Voting at Wellington in 2022 (Category D):

  • The DFAT Minute, "Australian federal election – 2022 window – Overseas voting risk analysis and recommendations" (20 December 2021), is the relevant document for this category.
  • The document's title confirms its purpose is to provide analysis and recommendations concerning overseas voting for the 2022 election.
  • While the specific details and rationale for the decision not to offer in-person voting at the Australian High Commission in Wellington for the 2022 federal election are extensively redacted under s 22(1)(a)(ii) of the FOI Act, the document's existence and title confirm that such a decision was made following an assessment of risks and recommendations. This aligns with the data showing zero in-person votes cast at Wellington in 2022.

In stark contrast, the Australian Consulate-General in Auckland continued to offer in-person voting in 2022 (749 votes), alongside postal voting. This creates an inequitable two-tiered system for Australian citizens within the same country, where access to voting rights is contingent on their specific location. Furthermore, the overall number of postal votes returned directly to the AEC from New Zealand addresses saw a dramatic decrease in 2022 (360) compared to previous elections (1,389 in 2019, 1,309 in 2016). When combined with the elimination of in-person voting in Wellington, this suggests a net reduction in the overall participation of Australians in New Zealand, or at least a significant shift to less accessible methods, potentially leading to disenfranchisement for those unable or unwilling to navigate the postal system.

Government Actions and Progressive Values:
The internal DFAT Minute, though heavily redacted, indicates that the decision to differentiate between High Commissions and Consulates-General, restricting in-person voting at the former, was a deliberate policy choice. The visible sections allude to "risk analysis," "staffing challenges," "risks of large numbers of people congregating" (likely COVID-19 related), "reputational risks," and "cost implications."

From a progressive standpoint, prioritizing operational convenience, perceived risk, or cost savings over the fundamental right to vote and equitable access to democratic participation is a significant deviation from core values. Governments, particularly those committed to social justice, should actively work to expand, not restrict, voter access. While health concerns during a pandemic are legitimate, the documents do not provide clear evidence that a robust, safe in-person voting option was unfeasible or that sufficient alternatives were adequately communicated and facilitated, especially given that Auckland successfully offered it. The implicit message is that the administrative burden or cost outweighed the imperative of ensuring maximum democratic participation for citizens overseas.

In summary, these documents highlight a governmental action that, while framed internally as a risk management and cost-efficiency measure, effectively curtailed civil liberties by restricting voter access and created social inequity in electoral participation for Australian citizens in New Zealand. This action deviates from progressive values that champion universal and equitable access to democratic processes as paramount.
