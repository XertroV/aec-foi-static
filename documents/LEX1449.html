FOI Request LEX1449

Date: 2022

FOI Request Summary

This summary outlines the details of FOI Request No. LEX1499, focusing on its purpose, the documents involved, and their main content.

Main Purpose of the FOI Request

The FOI request, identified as LEX1499, sought copies of all documents pertaining to the consideration of objections made by an applicant (referred to as "Applicant") in response to the Australian Electoral Commission's (AEC) advertisement of David Pocock's application to register as a non-Parliamentary political party in the Register of Political Parties.

Documents from the FOI Request

The primary document retrieved and provided in response to this FOI request is a "Minute: For action – Final determination of application made under s 126(1)(b) of the Commonwealth Electoral Act 1918 (Electoral Act) for registration as a non-Parliamentary party – David Pocock (the Party)." This minute is dated 18 March 2022.

Main Content from the FOI Request Documents

The minute details the process and considerations leading to the final determination of David Pocock's party registration application, specifically addressing the public objections received.

  • Application Background:

    • David Pocock's application for registration as a non-Parliamentary party was received by the AEC on 24 December 2021.
    • The application was advertised on 11 February 2022 on the AEC website and in 10 major newspapers, inviting written particulars (objections) by 11 March 2022.

  • Objections Received:

    • The AEC received four sets of written particulars from three objectors: "Ernst" (two objections), "Neil," and "Lis," all submitted on 10 March 2022.
    • Objections were required to specify belief that the application should be refused under reasons listed in s 132(2)(b) of the Electoral Act (e.g., party not eligible, application not in accordance with s 126, name/abbreviation tests under s 129).

  • Summary of Objections:

    • Ernst (Objection 1) & Neil: Raised concerns about David Pocock's eligibility for the Senate due to foreign citizenship (Zimbabwe) at the time of the party's application. Argued the AEC should not register a party named after an ineligible person and suggested re-submission upon eligibility. This related to s 44 of the Constitution.
    • Ernst (Objection 2) & Lis: Argued that a single person's name cannot constitute a "party," as a party implies a body of persons united by a cause or opinion. They contended that "David Pocock" is merely a name, not indicative of a political party or social cause, and suggested names like "David Pocock for the ACT" or "David Pocock for the environment" would be more appropriate. Both warned of potential "serious consequences" and election challenges if such a party were registered.
    • Neil: Also stated the name "David Pocock" was confusing as a party name, especially if appearing both above and below the line on a ballot paper.

  • Party's Response to Objections:

    • On 16 March 2022, the Party's Registered Officer, Mr. Glenn Cummings, responded to all objections.
    • General Response: Stated that nothing raised in the objections, under the Act, would prevent or permit the Electoral Commissioner from declining to register the Party.
    • Regarding the "single name" argument: Argued there is no requirement in the Act for a party name to be "consistent with the concept of a party" or include specific references like "party" or "group." It asserted that the Party's objects and operation meet the Act's requirements, and the name merely serves as identification. The objection invited a qualitative evaluation beyond the AEC's powers.
    • Regarding the "speculative election challenge": Dismissed it as a speculative allegation without grounding in any specific Act provision for invalidity.
    • Regarding the s 44 (citizenship/eligibility) argument: Emphasized that the eligibility of a proposed endorsed candidate for election is separate from the eligibility of a party to be registered. The Party meets the registration requirements, and candidate eligibility is not part of the party registration assessment, being relevant only at the point of candidate nomination.

  • AEC's Consideration and Conclusion:

    • The minute indicates that the AEC delegate, after considering both the objections and the Party's response, found "no additional grounds or information provided to give reason for the delegate to refuse the application for registration."
    • The minute concludes that "there is no basis to refuse the Application and the Application should be approved for registration."
    • A draft statement of reasons was prepared, recommending the Party's registration, in the interests of transparency.

Legislative framework – written particulars 
Section 132(2)(b) of the Electoral Act provides that a person can only submit written particulars 
objecting to an application to register a party (or a logo in the case of s 132(2)(b)(iv) of the Electoral 
Act) if the person believes that:  

a)  the application does not relate to an eligible political party; or 
b)  the application is not in accordance with s 126 of the Electoral Act 
(the section setting out the requisite contents of an application); or  

c)  the application should be refused under s 129 of the Electoral Act (the name and 

abbreviation tests); or 

d)  the Electoral Commission should refuse to enter a logo of the party in the Register under 

s 129A of the Electoral Act (the logo test). 

For the Application, written particulars are required to meet the following administrative requirements 
under ss 132(2)–(3) of the Electoral Act: 

1.  be in writing; 
2.  be signed by the objector (either physically, or electronically as per s 10(1) of the Electronic 

Transactions Act 1999); 

3.  specify a postal address of the objector that does not consist of a post office box number 
(noting the definition of ‘address’ in s 123(1) of the Electoral Act and the postal service 
requirements in s 140(1) of the Electoral Act); 

4.  be submitted within 1 month after the publication of the relevant s 132(1) Notice on the AEC 

website; and 

5.  set out the objector’s belief that the Application should be refused for a reason listed in 

s 132(2)(b) of the Electoral Act and provide grounds in support of that belief. 

Written particulars received objecting to the Application must be processed in accordance with 
ss 132(4) and (5) of the Electoral Act. This requires the Electoral Commission to make the written 
particulars available for public inspection (published on the AEC website) under s 132(4) of the 
Electoral Act, and provide a copy to the Registered Officer for an opportunity to reply under s 132(5) 
of the Electoral Act.

Consideration of the Objections and the Response 

Under ss 132A(1) and 133(3) of the Electoral Act, if the Electoral Commission refuses an application 
for registration, the Electoral Commission must: 

•  publicise the reasons for the Electoral Commission’s decision; and  
•  give written notice of those reasons to the applicant(s); and 
•  give written notice of those reasons to any person who submitted an objection under s 132(2) 

in relation to the application. 

Under ss 133(1) and 133(1A) of the Electoral Act, if the Electoral Commission makes a determination 
that a political party should be registered, the Electoral Commissioner must:  

•  publish notice of the registration on the AEC website (and may publish the notice in any other 

way that the Electoral Commissioner considers appropriate); 
•  give written notice of the registration to the applicant(s); and 
•  give written notice to any person who submitted an objection under s 132(2) in relation to the 

application, setting out the reasons for rejecting the person’s reasons for making their 
objection. 

You are a delegate of the Electoral Commission for the purposes of ss 132A(1), 133(1) and 133(3), 
and you are authorised to publish notice of registration under s 133(1A) of the Electoral Act. 
A copy of a draft statement of reasons (‘the Draft Statement of Reasons’) is at OBJECT ID: 
A1389673 and has been drafted on the recommendation that you make the determination that the 
Party should be registered. 

Although it would not be a legal requirement for you to publicise or provide reasons to the Applicants 
if you make the determination that the Party should be registered (as opposed to if you decide to 
refuse the Application), the Draft Statement of Reasons has been drafted in the interests of 
transparency and consistency.  

Conclusion 

On 24 December 2021, the AEC received all of the procedural requirements for an application for 
registration from the Applicants under ss 126(1)(b) and 126(2) of the Electoral Act.
