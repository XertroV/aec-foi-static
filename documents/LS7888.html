FOI Request LS7888

Date: 2020

The following is a summary of the FOI request LS7888 and its associated released documents:

Main Purpose of the FOI Request

The Freedom of Information (FOI) request LS7888 sought documents from January 1, 2018, to December 31, 2019, concerning the Australian Electoral Commission's (AEC) new methodology for its Indigenous Electoral Participation Program (IEPP). Specifically, the request aimed to uncover:
* Planning documents and the catalyst for the new methodology.
* Progress reports and internal communications regarding its design, testing, and implementation.
* Details on how implementation was tracked, including comparisons with prior methodologies.
* Final summary reports on the outcomes.
* Information on the number of Indigenous people involved in the planning, design, and implementation.
* The final cost of the methodology's design, testing, and implementation, including AEC staffer numbers.

Documents from the FOI Request

The released documents comprise a schedule of 41 items (some subsuming others) and several redacted document excerpts. The types of documents include:
* Internal Emails: Correspondence between various AEC staff members (Assistant Directors, Directors, Senior Project Officers, Consultants, Data Analysts) discussing Indigenous data, enrolment rates, parliamentary reviews, and ministerial briefings.
* Papers/Reports: Documents titled "Indigenous Franchise KPIs using Centrelink data" and "Analysis of Indigenous Enrolment."
* Ministerial Submissions & Briefs: Formal documents prepared for Ministers and parliamentary estimates hearings (e.g., "Ministerial Submission re Publication of Indigenous enrolment rates," "Estimates Brief – Hot Issue re Indigenous," "Estimates Brief – Hot Issue – Supplementary Budget Estimates").
* Redaction Codes List: A list detailing the types of information redacted from the documents.

Main Content from the FOI Request Documents

The core content of the released documents focuses heavily on the AEC's shift to a new, more direct methodology for estimating Indigenous electoral enrolment rates, primarily leveraging self-identified Indigeneity data from Centrelink (Department of Human Services).

Key Insights from the Documents:

  • Catalyst for New Methodology: The availability of person-level Centrelink extracts that included an Indigenous indicator from 2017 onwards was the primary catalyst. Previously, the AEC used an indirect regression model, which yielded "alarmingly low" and less robust Indigenous enrolment rates.
  • New Methodology Details: The new approach involves:
    • Calculating the proportion of Centrelink customers (with identified Indigenous or non-Indigenous status) who are on the electoral roll.
    • Adjusting these raw Centrelink-based rates to align with overall AEC enrolment rates, applying this factor to Indigenous and non-Indigenous data.
    • The methodology aims to provide more direct and robust estimates, though it still relies on assumptions (e.g., no bias in Centrelink client enrolment or matching for Indigenous/non-Indigenous individuals).
  • Improved Enrolment Rates: The new methodology revealed significantly higher estimated Indigenous enrolment rates:
    • As at June 30, 2018, the national Indigenous enrolment rate was estimated at 76.4%. This was a substantial increase from the previous estimates of 58% (calculated in 2014 using the old methodology).
    • As at June 30, 2019, the national rate remained steady at 76.6%, continuing an upward trend from 74.7% in 2017.
    • The estimated unenrolled Indigenous population was around 118,581 in June 2018, increasing slightly to 120,486 by June 2019.
  • Implementation Tracking & Enrolment Stimulation:
    • The Centrelink data identified 72,451 "potentially unenrolled Indigenous Australians" in July 2018.
    • An enrolment stimulation event in February/March 2019 resulted in 2,646 Indigenous Australians enrolling, reducing the identified unenrolled cohort to 69,143.
    • Internal discussions reveal efforts to refine the data analysis, particularly concerning older Indigenous Australians and potential matching errors.
    • There were internal discussions (emails from Sept 2019) about state-level variations, such as a noted increase in the unenrolled Indigenous population in NSW, though it was deemed not statistically significant given the state's size.
  • Reporting and Transparency: The AEC prepared ministerial submissions and estimates briefs to communicate these updated enrolment figures to the government and parliament, emphasizing the improved methodology and increased rates. Discussions noted the importance of clearly stating the estimated nature of the data ("estimates on top of estimates") due to the lack of a direct Indigenous identifier on the federal roll.

Information Not Explicitly Addressed in Released Content:

  • The documents do not explicitly outline the number of Indigenous people involved by the AEC in the planning, design, and implementation of the new methodology in terms of project personnel or consultants.
  • The documents also do not detail the final cost of the design, testing, and implementation of the new methodology, nor the specific number of AEC staffers assigned to the task. While staff names and roles appear in emails, a dedicated breakdown of staffing or costs for the project is not present in the provided snippets or document descriptions.

However, matching the Roll to Centrelink data allows a more direct method of estimating the

It is still subject to quite courageous assumptions, but as fewer are required it likely represents an

  For all Centrelink customers with an identified Indigenous or non-Indigenous status, 

calculate the proportion on the Roll. This is the raw enrolment rate of Centrelink clients. 
  Calculate the ratio of the actual AEC enrolment rate versus the Centrelink-based rate, for the 
same point in time. This is the overall factor to adjust any Centrelink-based rates to align

  Apply the adjustment factor to raw Centrelink-based enrolment rates by Indigenous status

  This process can be done at the State level, though results will have lower reliability. 

It should be noted that while this method avoids indirectly modelling Indigenous enrolment rates via 
geographic association, it does assume that any bias in Centrelink clients being enrolled is equal for 
Indigenous and non-Indigenous, and that there are no substantive Indigenous-status biases inherent 
in the Roll-to-Centrelink person matching. 

The Centrelink client file with Indigenous indicator newly available to the AEC has presented an 
excellent opportunity, via matching to the Roll and mark-off data, to improve estimation of 
Indigenous franchise KPIs.  

While the results are subject to a number of non-trivial assumptions, the more direct estimation

3. Applying this adjustment factor to the raw Centrelink-based Indigenous and non-Indigenous enrolment rates to produce adjusted, indicative estimates.
