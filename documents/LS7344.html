FOI Request LS7344

Date: 2019

FOI Request LS7344 Summary

Main Purpose of the FOI Request

The Freedom of Information (FOI) Request LS7344 sought "All correspondence about compliance with the Commonwealth Electoral Act 1918 between the Australian Election Commission and the US technology companies Facebook Inc. and Twitter Inc. since July 2017."

Documents from the FOI Request

The released documents specifically pertain to correspondence between the Australian Electoral Commission (AEC) and Twitter Inc. (Facebook Inc. correspondence is not included in this release). The schedule lists four key email documents, all exchanged in August 2018:

  • Document 1 (22/08/2018): Email from Kara Hinesley (Twitter) to AEC representatives (Evan Ekin-Smyth, Tim Courtney, Andrew Johnson) following up on a meeting.
  • Document 2 (22/08/2018): Email from Emily Foat (Twitter Australia) to Kara Hinesley (Twitter) offering a media workshop to the AEC.
  • Document 3 (23/08/2018): Email from Evan Ekin-Smyth (AEC) to Emily Foat and Kara Hinesley (Twitter) confirming receipt and indicating further follow-up.
  • Document 4 (30/08/2018): Email from Andrew Johnson (AEC Principal Government Lawyer) to Emily Foat and Kara Hinesley (Twitter) proposing detailed scenarios for compliance with the Commonwealth Electoral Act.

Main Content Related to the FOI Request

The correspondence details the AEC's efforts to establish a clear and efficient process with Twitter for ensuring compliance with the Commonwealth Electoral Act 1918, particularly regarding the authorisation of electoral communications on the platform.

Key content includes:

  • Initial Engagement (August 2018): The emails confirm an in-person meeting took place between AEC and Twitter representatives (Kara Hinesley, Emily Foat, Evan Ekin-Smyth, Tim Courtney, Andrew Johnson) to discuss handling issues during future campaigns and elections. Twitter offered to conduct a media workshop for the AEC to optimize platform use for campaigns.
  • Proposed Compliance Scenarios: Andrew Johnson of the AEC outlined three scenarios and proposed actions for Twitter's assistance in managing non-compliant electoral communications on the platform:
    1. Paid electoral advertisements without proper authorisation: The AEC would attempt to contact the responsible party first. If unsuccessful, they would notify Twitter, expecting Twitter to remove/block the ad or ensure its authorisation.
    2. Anonymous communications containing electoral matter: The AEC would try to ascertain if the responsible party is a "disclosure entity" (requiring authorisation). If this cannot be confirmed, Twitter would be asked to assist. If the party is a disclosure entity and fails to authorise, Twitter would be expected to remove/block the content. Twitter could confirm disclosure entity status without sharing personal information with the AEC.
    3. Electoral advertisements infringing other Electoral Act offences (e.g., misleading/deceptive content): The AEC would request information from Twitter about the person or entity responsible for the communication.
  • Legal Recourse: The AEC noted that if Twitter is unable to remove or block non-compliant communications or provide requested information, the AEC (or a candidate) may seek an injunction against the responsible party and/or Twitter under Section 383 of the Electoral Act.
  • Electoral Act Background: The AEC provided a summary of the Commonwealth Electoral Act 1918, explaining that Part XXA requires authorisation for electoral communications to inform voters who is responsible for the content, but it does not regulate the truth of the communications. Definitions for "electoral matter" and "disclosure entity" were also included.

As a politically left-leaning analyst, several issues arise:

Alignment with Progressive Values:
The AEC's stated primary concern, that "voters know who is communicating an electoral matter to them," aligns with the progressive value of transparency in democratic processes. Ensuring that political advertising and communications are authorized helps combat clandestine influence and allows citizens to understand the source of information, a foundational element for informed public participation.

Deviation from Progressive Values and Areas of Concern:

  1. Social Justice & Misinformation: The most significant deviation from progressive values is explicitly stated: "The Electoral Act does not regulate truth in electoral communications." This is a critical failure from a social justice perspective. While the AEC focuses on who is communicating, it allows for the unhindered spread of potentially misleading or deceptive information, provided it is authorized. This directly undermines the integrity of the electoral process and can disproportionately harm vulnerable communities through disinformation campaigns, which are often used to sow division, suppress votes, or spread discriminatory narratives. A progressive approach would advocate for mechanisms to address the accuracy and truthfulness of electoral content, not just its attribution.

  2. Corporate Influence & Wealth Distribution:

    • The documents reveal a dynamic where a powerful US technology corporation, Twitter, is actively engaged in shaping its regulatory environment. Twitter's representatives proactively offer a "Twitter media workshop to update you on Twitter's media solutions and showcase the best way to utilise the platform from a campaign perspective." This highlights the inherent power imbalance: a private, profit-driven entity offering "solutions" to a government regulator, rather than simply being subject to regulations. This suggests corporations are not just complying but are actively involved in determining the mechanisms of compliance, potentially to their own commercial advantage.
    • The focus on "paid electoral advertisements" underscores how financial resources can leverage corporate platforms to amplify political messages. While the AEC aims for transparency of authorship, the underlying issue of wealth influencing electoral discourse through paid content on these platforms remains unaddressed by the Act itself.

  3. Civil Liberties (Freedom of Speech vs. Public Interest):

    • The Act's narrow focus on authorization, rather than content accuracy, represents a particular interpretation of freedom of speech that prioritizes the speaker's right to disseminate information without government scrutiny over the public's right to accurate information in a democratic context. From a progressive viewpoint, absolute freedom of speech should not extend to deliberate disinformation that undermines public trust or manipulates democratic outcomes, particularly when propagated by powerful actors or through paid means.
    • The scenarios also involve Twitter being asked to "remove or block" non-compliant communications and, in more serious cases, to "provide the AEC the requested information" about responsible persons or entities. While this is necessary for enforcement, it vests significant power in a private company (Twitter) to act as a gatekeeper of political speech and to share user data with government, raising questions about accountability, transparency in content moderation decisions, and potential implications for privacy and surveillance if not strictly limited and overseen.

In summary, while the AEC's efforts to ensure transparency in electoral communication authorship align with basic democratic principles, the documents reveal a regulatory framework (and its application) that falls short of progressive ideals by failing to address the fundamental issue of misinformation, allowing powerful corporations to maintain significant influence over the digital public square, and potentially creating an environment where wealth can distort democratic discourse, all while operating under a narrow interpretation of civil liberties that prioritizes attribution over accuracy and the public good.

Background 
The Commonwealth Electoral Act 1918 (Electoral Act) is concerned that voters know who is 
communicating an electoral matter to them (electoral communication).  The Electoral Act does not 
regulate truth in electoral communications.  Part XXA of the Electoral Act requires electoral 
communications to have an authorisation to inform voters who is responsible for the 
communication.
