FOI Request LEX5098

Date: 2023

The FOI request primarily sought documents pertaining to the Australian Electoral Commission's (AEC) electoral roll management, with a particular focus on the integrity of the roll and the processes for its maintenance. The documents suggest the request aimed to understand how the AEC ensures the accuracy of the electoral roll, especially concerning the removal of deceased electors.

Documents from the FOI Request

The released documents comprise a range of internal AEC materials, including:
* Decision on Access pages: Indicating that most documents were released in part, primarily under exemptions for deliberative processes (Section 22) and personal privacy (Section 47F).
* "STATE OF THE ELECTORAL ROLL" reports (multiple versions from February, August, and October 2023): Providing overviews and statistics regarding the roll's status.
* "Briefing" documents and "Talking points" (e.g., "AEC Roll Integrity Assurance System Talking Points," "Electoral roll integrity Talking points" from July and October 2023): Outlining the AEC's strategies and processes for maintaining roll integrity.
* "Reporting on the Electoral Roll Results, Activity and Trend June Quarter 2023": A detailed report on additions, deletions, and objections.
* Internal email communications: Related to the preparation and distribution of briefing materials for AEC leadership and for parliamentary estimates.
* Flowchart: Visualising the "AEC Roll Integrity Assurance System."
* Agenda Paper for Executive Leadership Team: Discussing Quality Management System and past audits related to roll integrity.

Main Content from FOI Request Documents

The documents consistently highlight the AEC's robust and multi-faceted approach to maintaining the integrity and accuracy of the electoral roll:

  • Roll Integrity Assurance System: The AEC employs a comprehensive system that includes:

    • High Trust Data Sources: Utilising data from various government agencies (e.g., Australian Bureau of Statistics, Services Australia, Australian Taxation Office, National Exchange of Vehicle and Driver Information System (NEVDIS), Births, Deaths and Marriages registries, Australia Post) for identity verification and roll management, particularly for death deletions.
    • Human and System Checks: Regular internal "Roll Integrity Checks" by trained operators using system-generated lists to identify and correct anomalies such as duplicate enrolments, matching client details (e.g., "Electoral Twins"), and data entry errors. The system also conducts real-time analysis using 37 conditional rule checks on monthly roll extracts to identify potential fraud factors (e.g., same ID numbers used, voting history, patterns in applications from the same address).
    • Ongoing Maintenance Activities: Including merging duplicate records, processing objections, and maintaining the address register.
    • Electoral Event Updates: Information gathered during electoral events (e.g., Elector Information Reports from polling officials) is used to update the roll.

  • Removal of Deceased Electors: This is a key focus across several documents, detailing a "robust process" to prevent deceased persons from being added to the roll and to remove them.

    • The AEC collects and analyses information from various sources, including family advice, state Births, Deaths and Marriages (BDM) registrars, and other electoral authorities.
    • Death notifications from BDM are processed monthly.
    • The documents provide consistent statistics on the volume of deceased persons removed:
      • 153,000 in the 12 months ending 31 July 2023.
      • 145,000 in the 12 months ending 30 June 2023.
      • 156,000 in the 12 months ending 30 September 2023.
      • 148,500 in the 12 months ending 31 January 2023.
      • 147,700 in the 12 months ending 31 March 2023.

  • State of the Electoral Roll and Activity:

    • The enrolment rate is calculated monthly based on federal enrolments and the Estimated Eligible Population (EEP) from ABS data, adjusted for factors like migration, deaths, and citizenship changes.
    • The AEC estimates that over 7,000 people on the roll either move house or die daily, necessitating changes to their enrolment.
    • On average, 10,000 transactions are processed daily to add or remove people, or update details, ensuring the roll remains highly current and accurate.
    • As of June 2023, the federal enrolment stood at 17,565,728. Reports detail monthly additions, deletions, and net changes, often influenced by specific electoral events.

  • Oversight and Assurance:

    • The AEC is implementing a Quality Management System (QMS) to formalise its processes for roll management, including fraud investigation and de-duplication.
    • Internal audits, such as a 2013 review specifically on deceased electors, contribute to ongoing integrity. The flowchart also indicates external reviews by bodies like the ANAO and JSCEM.

The documents, despite significant redactions, consistently underline the AEC's proactive and systematic efforts to maintain a high-integrity and accurate electoral roll.

Deletions (duplicate, death, and eligible overseas electors) 

•  Removing electors from the roll to maintain accuracy and integrity is important to ensure the roll is 

always up to date. Deletions from the roll can occur for several reasons. Specifically, an 
enrolment can be deleted when: 

o  existing records are merged (if there are duplicate enrolment records) 
o  an elector has died 

•  Duplicate enrolment records occur when an elector’s name appears on the current electoral roll 
more than once. This can occur when a State Direct, Federal Direct, or ordinary enrolment 
application is processed without being matched to a current enrolment. The AEC investigates and 
corrects duplicate enrolments on a fortnightly basis.

OFFICIAL:SENSITIVE 

Deletions (duplicate, death, and eligible overseas electors) 

•  Removing electors from the roll to maintain accuracy and integrity is important to ensure the roll is 

always up to date. Deletions from the roll can occur for several reasons. Specifically, an 
enrolment can be deleted when: 

o  existing records are merged (if there are duplicate enrolment records) 
o  an elector has died 

•  Duplicate enrolment records occur when an elector’s name appears on the current electoral roll 
more than once. This can occur when a State Direct, Federal Direct, or ordinary enrolment 
application is processed without being matched to a current enrolment. The AEC investigates and 
corrects duplicate enrolments on a fortnightly basis.

OFFICIAL:SENSITIVE 

•  The AEC receives death notifications from a variety of sources including the registrar of Births 

Deaths and Marriages, electoral events, state and territory electoral authorities, Public Trustees, 
and members of the public. 

•  The AEC processes death notifications from Births Deaths and Marriages monthly; and receives 
notification directly from relatives and interested parties. Notifications are matched to current and 
deleted enrolment records (e.g. where a person has been removed from the roll by objection). 
Once matched with a death notification, further investigation is required before re-enrolment can 
occur. This process is an important integrity measure that reduces impersonation opportunity.

Background 

•  The AEC exports 30 roll extracts at the end of each month and applies 37 conditional rule 

checks to yield integrity scores. This real-time analysis is completed on individual elements 
or fraud factors including:  

o  same driver licence or passport or citizenship numbers used

o  number of applications submitted in the same street and locality 
o  number of applications submitted in the same electoral area

Background 

Current state of the roll 

• The enrolment rate is calculated on a monthly basis using federal enrolments and Estimated
Eligible Population (EEP) primarily based on Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) data. The
ABS information uses census-year populations by citizenship, updated for post-census
interstate and overseas migration, deaths, and population ageing, then projected to the
reference date and adjusted for new citizens, pre-1984 British Subjects, those who because of
unsound mind are unable to understand the nature and significance of voting, and overseas
electors.

2. Removal of deceased electors

• The AEC employs a robust process to remove deceased electors from the roll and prevent

deceased persons from being added to the electoral roll.

• The AEC manages the removal of deceased electors from the roll by collecting and

analysing information from a variety of sources, including advice from the elector’s family
and data from state registrars (Births, Deaths and Marriages), and other electoral
authorities.

•

In the 12 months ending 31 March 2023, the AEC removed 147,700 deceased persons
from the electoral roll.
